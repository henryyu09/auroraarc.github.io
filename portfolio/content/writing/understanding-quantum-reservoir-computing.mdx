## Why Reservoir Computing?

Reservoir computing is a fascinating approach to recurrent neural networks that flips the usual training paradigm on its head. Instead of training all the weights in a recurrent network — which is notoriously difficult due to vanishing gradients and long training times — you fix the recurrent connections and only train a simple linear readout layer.

The "reservoir" is a fixed, randomly initialized dynamical system. You feed inputs into it, let the dynamics evolve, and read out the system's state as a high-dimensional representation of your input. Because the reservoir is fixed, training is just linear regression or a simple classifier.

## The Quantum Advantage

Quantum reservoir computing (QRC) replaces the classical recurrent network with a quantum system — a set of interacting qubits whose dynamics are governed by quantum mechanics. The idea is that quantum systems can naturally explore an exponentially large Hilbert space, providing a richer feature space for classification.

Where classical reservoirs need to be carefully designed to have the right dynamical properties (echo state property, enough nonlinearity), quantum reservoirs get this "for free" from quantum mechanics itself.

## What I Built

In collaboration with QuEra Computing, I built a QRC model using their neutral-atom quantum architecture. The key implementation steps:

1. **Encoding**: Molecular features are encoded as initial Rydberg state populations of the atoms
2. **Evolution**: The system evolves under its natural Hamiltonian for a fixed time
3. **Measurement**: Final atomic states are measured, producing a feature vector
4. **Classification**: A linear SVM is trained on these feature vectors

## Key Lessons

- **Interaction time matters**: Too short and the reservoir doesn't mix enough; too long and dynamics become chaotic
- **Simulation vs hardware**: There's a meaningful gap (~5%) between simulated and hardware performance, highlighting the impact of noise
- **Connectivity matters**: The neutral-atom topology (which differs from superconducting qubits) requires careful reservoir design

This project gave me a deep appreciation for how algorithmic design must adapt to hardware constraints, and how quantum reservoir computing offers a practical path to quantum advantage on near-term devices.
